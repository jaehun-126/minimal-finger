Topology-to-Dimension Synthesis of a Structurally Minimal 3-DoF Closed-Chain Robotic Finger

Demonstration of the fabricated 3-DoF structurally minimal closed-chain robotic finger performing coordinated flexion/extension and abduction/adduction motions.

Abstract

Linkage-driven robotic fingers offer a distinct advantage by maintaining low inertia and mass at the distal segments while eliminating the inherent nonlinearities—such as friction, tendon slack, and hysteresis—commonly associated with tendon-driven systems. However, traditional anthropomorphic designs often decouple the joint mechanisms to mimic human anatomy, which inherently introduces an excessive number of links and joints beyond the mathematical minimum required, thereby drastically increasing structural complexity.

This research bypasses the conventional decoupled paradigm by treating the entire finger as a unified spatial closed-chain mechanism. We systematically enumerate admissible 3-DoF spatial closed-chain topologies, identify the minimum-link, minimum-joint family under mobility and palm-level actuation feasibility constraints, and then perform dimensional optimization using the NSGA-II algorithm to secure sufficient fingertip workspace under symmetry and anthropomorphic constraints.

Key Research Breakdown

Step 1: Topological Enumeration. 4 unique base graph topologies (Index 1-4) derived via simple graph and dual hypergraph mappings under structural minimization constraints.

Step 2: Dimensional Optimization. Multi-objective workspace maximization utilizing the NSGA-II algorithm, balanced with anthropomorphic boundary constraints.

Step 3: Functional Assessment. Physical prototype successfully executing 32 out of 33 grasp types in the GRASP taxonomy, ensuring high dexterity despite structural minimization.
