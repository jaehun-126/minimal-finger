Topology-to-Dimension Synthesis of a Structurally Minimal 3-DoF Closed-Chain Robotic Finger

Jaehun Kim1, Eunho Sung1, Seunghwan Byun1, Seungbin You2, Jaeheung Park3
1 Dept. of Intelligence and Information, Graduate School of Convergence Science and Technology, Seoul National University
2 Center for Humanoid Research, Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST)
3 Dept. of Intelligence and Information, Graduate School of Convergence Science and Technology, ASRI, RICS, Seoul National University
Under Review at IEEE Robotics and Automation Letters (RA-L)
Manuscript PDF

Demonstration of the fabricated 3-DoF structurally minimal closed-chain robotic finger. The mechanism successfully generates coordinated flexion/extension (FE) and abduction/adduction (AA) motions directly from palm-mounted actuators.

Abstract & Motivation

Linkage-driven robotic fingers offer a distinct advantage by maintaining low inertia at the distal segments while eliminating the inherent nonlinearities (e.g., friction, tendon slack) commonly associated with tendon-driven systems. However, traditional anthropomorphic designs often decouple the joint mechanisms to mimic human anatomy. This conventional approach inherently introduces an excessive number of links and joints beyond the mathematical minimum required for a 3-DoF motion, thereby drastically increasing structural complexity.

To address this dilemma, this research bypasses the decoupled paradigm by treating the entire finger as a unified spatial closed-chain mechanism. We systematically enumerate admissible 3-DoF spatial closed-chain topologies, identify the minimum-link and minimum-joint family under functional and actuation-feasibility constraints, and perform dimensional optimization to secure sufficient fingertip workspace bounded by anthropomorphic envelope limits.

End-to-End Computational Design Pipeline

Step 1: Topology Instantiation & Screening

Rather than relying on human intuition or mere trial-and-error, we systematically enumerate spatial closed-chain topologies yielding exactly 3-DoF. Applying Grübler's equation coupled with palm-level actuation-feasibility constraints, we isolate a minimal 7-link, 8-joint structural family.

  • Graph-based Enumeration: Links and joints are mapped to nodes and edges to mathematically filter out non-functional sub-mechanisms (e.g., S-S links causing uncontrollable axial spins).
  • Dual Hypergraph Mapping: The valid simple graphs are converted to hypergraphs where specific joint types (3R3U2S or 4R1U3S) are systematically allocated considering structural isomorphism.

Step 2: Dimensional Optimization

A structurally minimal closed-chain mechanism is highly coupled and inherently suffers from severely restricted workspaces. To resolve this, we formulate a multi-objective dimensional optimization problem leveraging the NSGA-II algorithm.

  • Multi-Objective Maximization: Simultaneously maximizing the voxelized 3D reachable volume (f1) and the pixelized 2D workspace area on the sagittal symmetry plane (f2).
  • Anthropomorphic Constraints: Hard geometric boundaries—such as monotonic structural progression (x1 ≤ x4 ≤ x9) and sagittal angle limits—are strictly imposed to prevent the solver from converging into mathematically optimal but physically non-humanoid (e.g., backward-bending) configurations.

Step 3: Physical Realization & Functional Validation

The Pareto-optimal geometry is translated into a physical prototype to validate its theoretical dexterity and mechanical robustness. A 4-finger anthropomorphic hand assembly demonstrates the integration of the unified mechanism.

  • Uncompromised Dexterity: Despite extreme structural minimization, the assembled hand successfully performs 32 of the 33 human grasp types categorized in the GRASP taxonomy.
  • Physical Robustness: Experimental evaluations confirm exceptional motion repeatability (average error of 0.01–0.03 mm) and practical fingertip payload capacity (~8.7 N under 300 mA control command).
