Topology-to-Dimension Synthesis of a Structurally Minimal 3-DoF Closed-Chain Robotic Finger

Jaehun Kim1, Eunho Sung1, Seunghwan Byun1, Seungbin You2, Jaeheung Park1
1 Seoul National University (SNU), Republic of Korea
2 Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST), Republic of Korea
Under Review at IEEE Robotics and Automation Letters (RA-L)
Manuscript PDF Code (Private)

Demonstration of the fabricated 3-DoF structurally minimal closed-chain robotic finger. The mechanism effectively generates coordinated flexion/extension (FE) and abduction/adduction (AA) motions directly from palm-mounted actuators.

Abstract & Motivation

Linkage-driven robotic fingers offer a distinct advantage by maintaining low inertia at the distal segments while eliminating the inherent nonlinearities (e.g., friction, tendon slack) commonly associated with tendon-driven systems. However, traditional anthropomorphic designs often decouple the joint mechanisms to mimic human anatomy. This conventional approach inherently introduces an excessive number of links and joints beyond the mathematical minimum required for a 3-DoF motion, thereby drastically increasing structural complexity.

To address this dilemma, this research bypasses the decoupled paradigm by treating the entire finger as a unified spatial closed-chain mechanism. We systematically enumerate admissible 3-DoF spatial closed-chain topologies, identify the minimum-link and minimum-joint family (7-link, 8-joint) under functional and actuation-feasibility constraints, and perform dimensional optimization to secure sufficient fingertip workspace under anthropomorphic boundary limits. The resulting mechanism places all actuators on the palm and successfully retains functional whole-hand grasp diversity despite its extreme structural minimality.

Computational Design Pipeline


Step 1: Topological Enumeration via Graph Theory. Extracting valid 7-link, 8-joint structures using simple graph constraints and dual hypergraph mappings to avoid unactuated axial spins (over-constraints).

Step 2: Dimensional Optimization. Maximizing voxelized 3D workspace and pixelized 2D symmetry-plane area using the NSGA-II algorithm, strictly bounded by anthropomorphic envelope constraints.

Step 3: Functional Validation. The assembled 4-finger prototype successfully performs 32 out of 33 human grasp types defined in the GRASP taxonomy, proving its dexterity.
