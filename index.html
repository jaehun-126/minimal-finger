Topology-to-Dimension Synthesis of a Structurally Minimal 3-DoF Closed-Chain Robotic Finger

Jaehun Kim1, Eunho Sung1, Seunghwan Byun1, Seungbin You2, Jaeheung Park3
1 Dept. of Intelligence and Information, Graduate School of Convergence Science and Technology, Seoul National University
2 Center for Humanoid Research, Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST)
3 Dept. of Intelligence and Information, Graduate School of Convergence Science and Technology, ASRI, RICS, Seoul National University

Abstract

Linkage-driven robotic fingers offer a distinct advantage by maintaining low inertia at the distal segments while eliminating the inherent nonlinearities—such as friction, tendon slack, and hysteresis—commonly associated with tendon-driven systems. However, traditional anthropomorphic designs often decouple the joint mechanisms to mimic human anatomy, which inherently introduces an excessive number of links and joints beyond the mathematical minimum required, thereby drastically increasing structural complexity.

This paper presents a computational design framework that bypasses the conventional decoupled paradigm by treating the finger as a unified spatial closed-chain mechanism. We systematically enumerate admissible 3-DoF spatial closed-chain topologies, identify the minimum-link/minimum-joint family under mobility and palm-level actuation feasibility constraints, and perform dimensional optimization to secure sufficient fingertip workspace under symmetry and anthropomorphic constraints. The resulting mechanism integrates abduction/adduction (AA) and flexion/extension (FE) motions directly from palm-mounted actuators. A physical prototype verifies that this minimal topology is kinematically admissible and capable of performing 32 of the 33 grasp types defined in the GRASP taxonomy.

End-to-End Computational Design Pipeline

Step 1: Topology Instantiation via Graph Theory

To ensure structural minimality, we treat the robotic finger as a kinematic chain graph. We perform an exhaustive search for 3-DoF spatial topologies that satisfy specific mobility requirements while minimizing total complexity.

  • Kinematic Constraint Filtering: We apply Grübler’s criterion to filter potential chain configurations, effectively discarding over-constrained mechanisms or those with redundant degrees of freedom that do not contribute to functional mobility.
  • Structural Minimization: By utilizing simple graph and hypergraph representations, we identify the minimal set of links (n=7) and joints (E=8) that preserve the required 3-DoF behavior. This prevents the inclusion of unnecessary passive joints often found in biomimetic designs.
  • Actuation Feasibility: We map the input space to the joint space to ensure all three input axes can be conveniently grounded at the palm, establishing a direct geometric mapping between the actuators and the distal output motion.

Step 2: Dimensional Optimization

Once a minimal topology is selected, its physical dimensions must be optimized to achieve anthropomorphic functionality. We employ the NSGA-II (Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II) to navigate the multi-dimensional design space.

We maximize the reachable workspace volume and ensure the finger follows an anatomically realistic trajectory. Hard constraints are applied to link ratios to maintain a human-like aesthetic and collision-free operation throughout the flexion/extension range.

Step 3: Functional Validation

The synthesized design is validated through both simulation and hardware prototyping. The minimal 7-link architecture is stress-tested to ensure it can withstand the forces required for stable object grasping.

As a final performance metric, we map the prototype's capability against the GRASP taxonomy. The results demonstrate that our minimal design maintains high dexterity, covering 32 out of 33 defined grasp patterns, proving that structural reduction does not sacrifice functional versatility.
